Quantum Leap in Green Energy: A Jurisprudential Challenge

A consortium of researchers in Dhaka and Delft claim a fivefold gain in photovoltaic efficiency — and inadvertently raise questions the Hague has not yet thought to ask.

By Adiba Rahman  |  Senior Correspondent Dhaka · Delft

Dhaka — A pioneering study published this week in Nature Energy describes a photovoltaic architecture that combines perovskite overlayers with a tandem silicon substrate, and reports conversion efficiencies long thought to belong to the far side of the decade.

The finding is, on its face, an engineering triumph. Read sideways, it is something considerably stranger: an accidental provocation to international environmental law. If a single Bangladeshi–Dutch laboratory can supply the energy of a small country at a fraction of the capital outlay, what becomes of the treaties that apportion carbon quotas by nation?

“We have, in effect, invented a fact that no existing regime knows how to count,” said Dr. Nadira Hossain, the project’s co-lead and a former fellow of the Hague Academy. “The Paris framework assumes scarcity. We have just built abundance.”

“The Paris framework assumes scarcity. We have just built abundance.”

A measurement, not a promise

Dr. Jan van der Bos, the Dutch co-lead, urged caution. The efficiencies, he said, are “reproducible in principle, not yet at industrial scale,” and ought to be read less as a commercial announcement than as a proof-of-concept. Industrial reality, he noted, has a way of reducing laboratory numbers by a factor or two. Even so, the numbers in question are dramatic enough that the reduction leaves a remainder still worth taking seriously.

What the consortium has measured, in essence, is a thermodynamic shortcut: a device architecture in which two absorbing layers are tuned to harvest different portions of the solar spectrum without the usual losses at their interface. The physics is not new; the engineering is. The trick, as the preprint describes it, lies in the annealing step that knits the layers together.

The quiet corridor at the UNFCCC

In Bonn, the reaction has been more political. Delegates to the spring intersessional session of the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change are already briefing their capitals on what a nation-state is, for purposes of the Convention, when it can supply its neighbours with power at cost. The question, at first blush, is a technical one about accounting — but the questions behind the question are older and less tractable.

Counsel for the Republic of Vanuatu, which brought the first trans-boundary carbon liability case before the International Court of Justice last year, observed drily that “abundance, in treaty law, is always somebody’s problem.” It is, for the present, unclear whose.

What it means for Bangladesh

For Dhaka, the implications are at once practical and rhetorical. A country that has spent the better part of two decades arguing for climate justice finds itself, unexpectedly, in a position to deliver it — provided the industrial pipeline can be built, financed, and defended against the patent thicket that will certainly follow. None of these conditions is guaranteed.

The Prime Minister’s office, approached for comment, declined to be drawn. A senior civil servant, speaking on condition of anonymity, allowed that “the laboratory has put the Republic in an awkward position, which is to say, a hopeful one.”

❦

The question now traveling down the corridors of the UNFCCC is whether abundance, arriving ahead of schedule and outside the developed world, will be treated as the windfall it plainly is — or as something more awkward. The answer, when it arrives, will be written as much by lawyers as by engineers.

It will also, one suspects, be read in these pages first.
