The Lead · Science & Jurisprudence

Quantum Leap in Green Energy:
A Jurisprudential Challenge

A consortium of researchers in Dhaka and Delft claim a fivefold gain in photovoltaic efficiency — and inadvertently raise questions the Hague has not yet thought to ask.
By Adiba Rahman  |  Senior Correspondent

Dhaka — A pioneering study published this week in Nature Energy describes a photovoltaic architecture that combines perovskite overlayers with a tandem silicon substrate, and reports conversion efficiencies long thought to belong to the far side of the decade.

The finding is, on its face, an engineering triumph. Read sideways, it is something considerably stranger: an accidental provocation to international environmental law. If a single Bangladeshi–Dutch laboratory can supply the energy of a small country at a fraction of the capital outlay, what becomes of the treaties that apportion carbon quotas by nation?

“We have, in effect, invented a fact that no existing regime knows how to count,” said Dr. Nadira Hossain, the project’s co-lead and a former fellow of the Hague Academy. “The Paris framework assumes scarcity. We have just built abundance.”

Abundance, one suspects, will not be treated kindly. The scholars convening at the UNFCCC’s spring intersessional in Bonn are already briefing late into the evening on what a nation-state is, for purposes of the Convention, when it can supply its neighbours with power at cost.

In Delft, the atmosphere is more measured. Dr. Jan van der Bos, the Dutch co-lead, maintains that the efficiencies are “reproducible in principle, not yet at industrial scale,” and that the work ought to be read less as a commercial announcement than as a proof-of-concept.

The question now traveling down the corridors of the UNFCCC is whether abundance, arriving ahead of schedule and outside the developed world, will be treated as the windfall it plainly is — or as something more awkward. → p. 3

Editorial · The Polymath’s View

On the Usefulness of Thinking Across the Grain

This publication was founded on a quiet conviction: that the most consequential problems of our century sit in the seams between disciplines — where the climate scientist must read like a jurist, where the jurist must read like a philosopher, and where the philosopher must, at last, read like a child.

The past week furnishes a neat illustration. A laboratory announces a breakthrough; a court, half a continent away, is asked to adjudicate its implications; a philosopher, three centuries dead, supplies the vocabulary in which the question is ultimately framed. These are not three stories. They are one story, told badly in three places.

We propose, modestly, to tell it once, and well.

— The Editors
